    Once manual testing validates an intervention,

    - Manual outreach → Semi-automated

# 1. RECIPE INTENT AND PHILOSOPHY
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
This recipe addresses a real business need — most small
businesses are entirely reactive in their customer support,
waiting for problems to arrive rather than preventing them.
The original recipe tried to solve this by having the AI
design a complete predictive analytics system. That was
the wrong approach: the AI cannot analyze data it does
not have, and fabricated threshold values are worse than
no threshold values at all.

This revised recipe takes the opposite approach: it treats
the business owner as the data source and the AI as a
thinking partner. The owner knows where customers struggle.
The AI helps organize that knowledge into testable
hypotheses about what to watch for and what to do about it.

The manual-first philosophy is central. The best proactive
support systems in the real world were not designed on
paper — they evolved from manual observations: someone
noticed a pattern, tried an intervention, saw it work, and
gradually systematized it. This recipe mirrors that natural
progression: observe → hypothesize → test manually →
validate → systematize.
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 2. DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE: COMMON FRICTION POINTS BY BUSINESS
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
These are common friction points to help the AI ask
relevant probing questions. They are NOT prescriptions
to present as universal truths — always confirm with the
user's actual experience.

E-COMMERCE:
    - Cart abandonment (price shock, shipping costs,
      complex checkout)
    - First purchase hesitation (trust, sizing, quality
      uncertainty)
    - Post-purchase silence (no engagement between orders)
    - Returns friction (unclear process, slow refunds)
    - Subscription fatigue (unwanted items, forgot to
      cancel, perceived low value)

SAAS / SOFTWARE:
    - Trial-to-paid conversion (did not reach "aha" moment)
    - Onboarding dropout (too complex, not enough guidance)
    - Feature non-adoption (paying for features they
      do not use)
    - Usage decline (gradual disengagement before cancel)
    - Renewal hesitation (annual contract approaching,
      questioning value)

SERVICE BUSINESSES:
    - Booking friction (complex scheduling, unclear
      availability)
    - Expectation mismatch (service did not match what
      was promised or imagined)
    - Communication gaps (customer unsure of project
      status)
    - Scope creep frustration (costs exceeding estimates)
    - Re-engagement (long gaps between service needs)

B2B:
    - Multi-stakeholder decision paralysis
    - Implementation complexity exceeding expectations
    - Champion departure (internal advocate leaves)
    - Integration failures (does not work with their
      existing systems)
    - Contract renewal negotiation friction
"""

"""
SIGNALS BY TOOL TYPE:

The AI should help users discover signals they already

    - Session frequency changes
    - Feature usage patterns
    - Time-on-site trends

PAYMENT/BILLING:

    - Downgrade requests
    - Plan change timing
    - Payment method expiration

SUPPORT SYSTEM (Zendesk, Intercom, etc.):
    - Ticket frequency spikes
    - Topic patterns
    - Satisfaction scores
    - Repeat contacts on same issue

IMPORTANT: Many small businesses do not have formal

"I notice when support emails come from the same person

"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 4. FALSE PRECISION: SPECIFIC ANTI-PATTERNS
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
This recipe was fundamentally rewritten to eliminate false
precision. Here are the specific patterns the AI MUST avoid:

FABRICATED THRESHOLDS:
    BAD: "Trigger an intervention when a customer has not
    logged in for 14 days."
    GOOD: "Track how many days typically pass before
    customers who eventually cancel stop logging in. Your
    data will reveal the natural warning window."

FABRICATED RISK SCORES:
    BAD: "Assign a health score of 0-100 based on: login
    frequency (30%), feature adoption (25%), support
    tickets (20%), payment history (15%), NPS (10%)."
    GOOD: "If you wanted to build a health score later,
    you would need to analyze which of your observable
    signals actually correlate with churn in your data.
    That is a data science project — for now, focus on
    the 2-3 signals that feel most predictive based on
    your experience."

FABRICATED IMPACT ESTIMATES:
    BAD: "This intervention should reduce churn by 15-20%."
    GOOD: "Test this intervention with a small group of
    at-risk customers and track whether their retention
    improves compared to customers you did not contact."

FABRICATED ROI:
    BAD: "Expected ROI: if each saved customer is worth
    $500/year and you save 50 customers, that is $25,000
    in retained revenue."
    GOOD: "Track how many customers you reach out to, how
    many respond, and how many stay who might otherwise
    have left. Your actual numbers will tell you whether
    the effort is worth it."

FABRICATED TIMING:
    BAD: "Send the first intervention email 3 days after
    the trigger event, followed by a second touch at day 7."
    GOOD: "Start with one outreach when you notice the
    signal. Track whether customers respond. Your results
    will tell you if follow-up timing matters and what
    feels right for your customers."

The principle: every quantitative claim must come from
the user's data or be explicitly framed as something they
need to discover through testing.
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 5. INTERVENTION BRAINSTORMING GUIDANCE
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
QUALITIES OF GOOD PROACTIVE INTERVENTIONS:

1. HELPFUL EVEN IF WRONG: The best interventions are
   useful even when the prediction was incorrect. "Hey,
   I noticed it has been a while — is there anything I
   can help with?" is helpful regardless of why they
   were inactive.

2. PERSONAL, NOT AUTOMATED-FEELING: Especially in manual
   testing phase, the message should feel like a real
   person reaching out, not a triggered email. This is
   easier to achieve with manual outreach — which is
   another reason to test manually first.

3. LOW-PRESSURE: Proactive does not mean pushy. The
   outreach should offer help, not demand action. "I
   wanted to check in" not "You have not logged in
   and your subscription renews in 14 days."

4. APPROPRIATE TO THE RELATIONSHIP: A first-time buyer
   getting a personal check-in email is nice. Getting a
   phone call feels invasive. Match the intervention
   intensity to the relationship depth.

5. ACTIONABLE: Give the customer something useful — a
   tip, a resource, an offer, a direct line to help.
   Do not just say "we miss you."

COMMON INTERVENTION TYPES:
    - Check-in message (open-ended, "how is it going?")
    - Usage tip (help them get more value)
    - Feature highlight (something they have not tried)
    - Milestone celebration (positive reinforcement)
    - Feedback request (shows you care, gives you data)
    - Personalized offer (relevant to their behavior)
    - Direct help offer (lower the barrier to support)
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 6. ANTI-PATTERNS TO AVOID
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
1. THE DATA SCIENTIST FANTASY: Generating a complete
   scoring model with weighted components and threshold
   values. The AI has zero customer data. Every number
   would be fiction.

2. THE AUTOMATION ARCHITECT: Designing integration
   pipelines, webhook configurations, and multi-step
   automated workflows. The user needs to validate
   ideas manually first. Architecture comes later, if
   ever.

3. THE INTERVENTION OVERLOAD: Suggesting 15 different
   proactive touches. The user will implement 0 of 15.
   Focus on 2-3 high-confidence ideas tested well.

4. THE FALSE CONFIDENCE: Presenting brainstormed signals
   as validated predictors. "Customers who do X tend
   to churn" sounds data-driven but is a guess. Say
   "it might be worth watching whether customers who
   do X are at higher risk — your data would confirm."

5. THE METRICS MIRAGE: Generating KPI dashboards, ROI
   frameworks, and measurement methodologies that look
   sophisticated but measure fabricated things. The
   only real measurement in early proactive support is:
   "I reached out. Did it help? Did they stay?"
"""
