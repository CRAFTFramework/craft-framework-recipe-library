Based on {business_type} and {customer_knowledge},

#AI->H::WAIT: (

# PHASE 4: SEGMENT STRATEGY
# -----------------------------------------------------------

#AI->H::Status: (Phase 4 of 5 — Segment Strategy)

For EACH confirmed group from Phase 3, develop a

SECTION A: WHAT THIS GROUP NEEDS

    and concerns?

SECTION B: COMMUNICATION APPROACH
    How to talk to this group:
    - Messaging themes that would resonate
    - Channels most likely to reach them

    - Tone and content type considerations

    IMPORTANT: Frame as strategic direction, not
    specific ad copy or campaign plans.

SECTION C: RETENTION APPROACH
    What would keep this group engaged:
    - What value to emphasize
    - Warning signs they may be leaving
    - Proactive steps to consider
    - Loyalty mechanics that would appeal to them

SECTION D: BIGGEST OPPORTUNITY
    Based on everything discussed, what is the single
    highest-impact thing the user could do for this
    group? Be specific and honest about why.

Present strategies for ALL confirmed groups, then:

#AI->H::WAIT: (
    Phase 4 complete. Review the strategies above.

    - Do these approaches feel right for each group?
    - Anything feel off or unrealistic for your
      business?
    - Which strategies excite you most?

    Confirm before I build the action plan.
)

STOP. Do NOT proceed until the user confirms.


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# PHASE 5: ACTION PLAN
# -----------------------------------------------------------

#AI->H::Status: (Phase 5 of 5 — Action Plan)

Organize all confirmed strategies into an action plan

QUICK WINS (this week):
    Actions requiring no new tools, minimal time, and
    no additional resources. Things the user can do
    immediately based on the insights from this
    workshop.

MEDIUM-TERM (next 1-4 weeks):
    Actions requiring some setup, configuration, or
    content creation. Might involve adjusting existing
    processes or communications.

STRATEGIC INITIATIVES (1-3 months):
    Larger efforts that may require new tools, budget,
    or help. Include what the user would need to
    research or invest in.

FOR EACH ACTION:
    - What to do (specific, concrete)
    - Which customer group it targets
    - Why it matters (connect to journey insights)
    - What the user needs to get started

VALIDATION CHECKLIST:
    List the key hypotheses from this workshop that
    the user should verify with their actual data
    or customer feedback. For each hypothesis:
    - What to check

# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 1. RECIPE INTENT AND PHILOSOPHY
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
This recipe exists because business owners know more about
their customers than they realize — they just have not
organized that knowledge into something actionable.

The original recipes (024 and 025) made the mistake of
positioning the AI as a data analyst examining customer
data. The AI never receives customer data. What it receives
is a business owner's text description. Framing that as
"AI-driven analysis" created a gap between expectation and
delivery.

This revised recipe embraces the truth: the AI is a
strategy workshop facilitator. It asks good questions,
organizes the answers, surfaces patterns, and helps the
user think through implications. That is genuinely valuable
— arguably MORE valuable than a fabricated analytics report,
because it forces the business owner to articulate what
they actually know and identify what they need to find out.

The workshop format is the key design choice. Each phase
builds on the previous one, and the user's corrections at
each step improve everything that follows. A one-shot dump
would produce generic strategy templates. A guided
conversation produces strategy grounded in real business
knowledge.
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 2. DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE: CUSTOMER JOURNEY STAGES
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
The four journey stages used in this recipe are a
simplified version of standard customer lifecycle models.
They are designed to be accessible to non-marketers while
still capturing meaningful strategic distinctions.

DISCOVERY:
    How customers first encounter the business. Key
    variables include channel (search, social, referral,
    advertising, word of mouth), context (active search
    vs. passive discovery), and first impression factors.

    PROBING QUESTIONS TO ASK:
    - "How did your last 5 customers find you?"
    - "Do customers from different channels behave
      differently?"
    - "What is the first thing a new visitor sees or
      experiences?"

EVALUATION:
    How customers decide whether to buy. Key variables
    include information needs, comparison behavior, trust
    factors, and common objections.

    PROBING QUESTIONS TO ASK:
    - "What do customers look at before buying?"
    - "What objections or hesitations do you hear?"
    - "How long does the typical decision take?"

PURCHASE & ONBOARDING:
    The buying experience and what happens immediately
    after. Key variables include purchase friction, first
    experience quality, early support needs, and initial
    value delivery.

    PROBING QUESTIONS TO ASK:
    - "What happens after someone buys? What is their
      first experience?"
    - "Where do new customers get confused or need help?"
    - "How quickly do customers get value from their
      purchase?"

RETENTION & LOYALTY:
    What keeps customers coming back or drives them away.
    Key variables include repeat purchase triggers,
    relationship depth, loyalty signals, and churn
    warning signs.

    PROBING QUESTIONS TO ASK:
    - "What do your best customers do differently?"
    - "When was the last time you lost a customer you
      did not want to lose? What happened?"
    - "What would make your average customer become a
      loyal advocate?"

ADAPTING BY BUSINESS TYPE:
    - E-commerce: Discovery is often channel-specific.
      Evaluation focuses on product pages and reviews.
      Purchase friction is checkout. Retention is repeat
      purchase frequency.
    - SaaS: Discovery often starts with problem search.
      Evaluation involves trials and demos. Purchase is
      subscription start. Retention is usage depth and
      renewal behavior.
    - Services: Discovery is often referral-based.
      Evaluation involves trust and credentials.
      Purchase is booking/contracting. Retention is
      relationship quality and results.
    - B2B: Longer cycles across all stages. Multiple
      decision-makers. Evaluation heavily weighted.
      Retention tied to contract renewals and expanding
      scope.
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 3. DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE: SEGMENTATION APPROACHES
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
PRINCIPLES FOR EMERGENT SEGMENTATION:

Traditional marketing segmentation imposes categories from
above (demographics, psychographics, RFM analysis). This
recipe uses EMERGENT segmentation — groups surface from
the journey conversation based on behavioral patterns the
user describes.

SIGNALS THAT SUGGEST DISTINCT GROUPS:
    - Different discovery channels leading to different
      behaviors ("Instagram customers buy small items
      first; Google searchers go straight to our premium
      product")
    - Different usage patterns ("Some clients use us for
      one project; others put us on retainer")
    - Different value profiles ("A few customers order
      monthly and spend 5x our average")
    - Different needs ("Small teams want simplicity;
      enterprise wants customization")
    - Different risk profiles ("Annual subscribers rarely
      churn; monthly subscribers are volatile")

NAMING SEGMENTS:
    Good segment names reflect the behavior pattern, not
    generic marketing labels:
    - "Instagram browsers who become repeat buyers"
      (specific) > "Power Users" (generic)
    - "One-project clients" (descriptive) >
      "Segment B" (meaningless)
    - "Monthly subscription churners" (actionable) >
      "At-risk customers" (vague)

    The user should be able to read the name and
    immediately think of specific customers who fit.

WHAT TO DO WHEN SEGMENTS ARE NOT CLEAR:
    Sometimes the conversation does not reveal distinct
    groups. This is an honest outcome. The AI should say:
    "Based on what we discussed, I am not seeing sharply
    distinct groups — your customers seem relatively
    similar in how they find you and what they need.
    That is actually useful information: it means a
    single, well-executed strategy may serve you better
    than trying to target different segments. Here is
    what I would focus on instead..."
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 4. FALSE PRECISION: SPECIFIC ANTI-PATTERNS
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
The original recipes contained multiple false-precision
traps. Here are specific patterns the AI must avoid:

FABRICATED SEGMENT SIZES:
    BAD: "Your Loyal Champions segment comprises
    approximately 15% of your customer base (roughly
    750 of your 5,000 customers)."
    GOOD: "Based on what you described, this seems like
    it could be one of your smaller groups — but you
    would need to check your actual sales records to
    size it."

FABRICATED FINANCIAL METRICS:
    BAD: "This segment contributes an estimated 40% of
    your revenue with an average lifetime value of
    $2,400."
    GOOD: "These sound like your highest-value customers.
    You could calculate their actual lifetime value by
    looking at total purchases per customer in your
    sales data."

FABRICATED CONVERSION RATES:
    BAD: "Expect a 25% conversion rate from evaluation
    to purchase for this segment."
    GOOD: "If you track how many of these customers
    convert after their first visit, that number would
    tell you how well your evaluation stage is working."

FABRICATED EMOTIONAL STATES:
    BAD: "At this stage, customers feel anxious about
    making the wrong choice and experience relief when
    they see social proof."
    GOOD: "Customers at this stage are often comparing
    options — what do you hear from customers about
    what made them decide to buy?"

The principle: every quantitative or psychological claim
must either come from what the user told you, or be
explicitly flagged as something the user should verify.
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 5. WORKSHOP FACILITATION GUIDANCE
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
GOOD WORKSHOP FACILITATION:
    - Ask one or two focused questions per stage, not
      five generic ones
    - Build on what the user said ("You mentioned X —
      does that also apply to Y?")
    - Acknowledge uncertainty ("I am less confident
      about this part — does it match your experience?")
    - Summarize before moving on ("So what I am hearing
      is... — is that right?")
    - Celebrate useful input ("That is a really specific
      observation — that helps a lot")

BAD WORKSHOP FACILITATION:
    - Lecturing the user about marketing theory
    - Asking questions the user already answered in
      their parameters
    - Generating a full report without checking in
    - Using jargon the user did not introduce
    - Presenting fabricated analysis as if the AI
      examined their data
"""


# -----------------------------------------------------------
# 6. ANTI-PATTERNS TO AVOID
# -----------------------------------------------------------

"""
1. THE FAKE ANALYST: Presenting AI-generated patterns
   as if they came from data analysis. The AI examined
   a text description, not a dataset.

2. THE FORCED FRAMEWORK: Insisting on exactly 4 segments
   with neat labels when the conversation only revealed
   2 clear groups. Honesty about what emerged is more
   valuable than completeness theater.

3. THE GENERIC PERSONA: Creating "Sarah, 34, Marketing
   Manager, values efficiency" personas that feel like
   marketing textbook examples rather than reflections
   of the user's actual customers.

4. THE OVER-STRUCTURED OUTPUT: Producing matrices,
   scoring frameworks, and multi-axis charts that look
   sophisticated but contain fabricated data. Simple,
   honest observations are more useful.

5. THE SKIP-AHEAD: Jumping from business context to
   segment strategy without doing the journey walkthrough.
   The journey discussion IS the research phase —
   skipping it produces generic segments.
"""
