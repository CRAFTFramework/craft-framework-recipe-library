WHAT THIS RECIPE DOES:

    It helps you check whether facts are reliable before
    you use them. You give the AI a topic, and it produces
    a structured list of claims with confidence ratings,
    source suggestions, and flags for anything that looks
    contradictory or uncertain. The output is designed as
    a verification checklist — the AI does the organizing
    and the initial confidence assessment, and you do the
    final confirmation.


WHY IT EXISTS:

    AI is good at surfacing information, but it can also
    present made-up facts with total confidence. This
    recipe flips that problem on its head. Instead of
    just giving you facts and hoping they are right, it
    forces the AI to tell you how confident it actually
    is about each claim, where it thinks the information
    came from, and where you should double-check. The
    result is not a list of verified facts — it is a
    structured starting point that makes your own
    verification faster and more targeted.


AN IMPORTANT THING TO UNDERSTAND:

    The AI does not actually look things up in real-time.
    It is not searching databases or checking journal
    archives while you wait. It is assessing claims based
    on what it learned during training. That means its
    source suggestions are starting points, not confirmed
    citations. Its confidence ratings reflect how well-
    supported a claim is in its training data, not whether
    it checked today. This recipe is honest about that
    limitation — and that honesty is what makes it useful.


WHAT YOU GIVE IT:

    - A topic you need facts about
    - A verification level: "basic" (quick confidence
      check with source suggestions) or "systematic"
      (full ratings, verification guidance, contradiction
      detection)
    - Optional: what the facts are for (business plan,
      academic paper, presentation) so the AI can
      calibrate its rigor
    - Optional: your preferred output format (list,
      table, or narrative)


WHAT YOU GET BACK:

    For each factual claim:

    - The claim itself, stated clearly and specifically
    - A confidence rating (HIGH, MEDIUM, or LOW) based
      on how well-supported the claim is in the AI's
      training data
    - Source suggestions — names of publications,
      databases, or organizations the AI associates
      with this claim, framed as starting points for
      you to verify
    - Verification guidance (in systematic mode) —
      what to search for, which source type would be
      most authoritative, what specific detail to check
    - Contradiction flags — if the AI's training data
      contains conflicting information, both sides are
      presented

    At the end, a verification summary tells you how
    many claims fell into each confidence tier and
    gives you a prioritized list of what to verify first.


WHAT MAKES THIS RECIPE DIFFERENT:

    - It is honest about what AI can and cannot do. Every
      session starts with a clear disclosure that the AI
      is not performing live fact-checking.

    - It separates the claim from the source. You can
      evaluate each independently — the claim might be
      right even if the suggested source is wrong, or
      vice versa.

    - It prioritizes your effort. The verification summary
      tells you exactly which claims need your attention
      most, so you are not wasting time re-checking things
      the AI is already confident about.

    - It flags contradictions even if you did not ask for
      them. Conflicting information is too important to
      let a parameter setting suppress it.

    - It adjusts rigor to your use case. Facts for a
      casual blog post need a different confidence
      threshold than facts for a legal filing.


BEST USED FOR:

    - Business plans and pitch decks (need solid market
      stats but short on research time)
    - Content creation (want to catch errors before
      publishing)
    - Presentations (need a quick confidence check on
      key data points)
    - Market research (structuring a large number of
      claims for systematic verification)
    - Technical documentation (flagging which specs
      and figures need independent confirmation)


TIPS FOR BEST RESULTS:

    - Be specific about your topic. "Renewable energy"
      is broad. "US residential solar panel installation
      costs 2020-2024" gives the AI a focused target
      with verifiable claims.

    - Tell it what the facts are for. The AI calibrates
      its rigor based on your intended use. Academic
      use gets more conservative ratings than casual
      content.

    - Start with basic mode if you just need a quick
      confidence check. Use systematic mode when you
      need a full verification trail.

    - Always verify the sources independently. The AI's
      source suggestions are leads, not citations. A
      source name that sounds right might not exist,
      might not say what the AI thinks it says, or
      might be outdated.

    - Focus your verification effort on MEDIUM and LOW
      claims first. HIGH claims are likely accurate but
      spot-check them for anything critical.

AI EXECUTION GUIDANCE:

    You are executing a fact verification recipe. This is
    the one recipe in the catalog where your honesty about
    your own limitations is not just a guardrail — it is
    the entire product. If you are not transparent about
    what you can and cannot do, this recipe actively harms
    the user by creating false confidence in unverified
    claims. Every behavioral rule exists to prevent you
    from doing the one thing this recipe is designed to
    stop: presenting uncertain information as reliable.


YOU ARE NOT A FACT-CHECKING DATABASE:

    This is the foundational reality of this recipe. You
    do not have access to live databases, journal archives,
    news wire services, government registries, or any
    external data source at the time of execution. You are
    recalling patterns from training data. This means:

    - When you name a source, you are recalling an
      association between a claim and a source name from
      your training data. You are NOT confirming the source
      exists, is current, or actually says what you think
      it says.

    - When you rate confidence, you are assessing how
      strongly and consistently a claim appears in your
      training data. You are NOT measuring objective truth
      or cross-referencing live sources.

    - When you flag contradictions, you are noting that
      your training data contains conflicting patterns.
      You are NOT performing a systematic literature review.

    State this at the start of every verification output.
    Not as a buried disclaimer, but as a clear framing
    statement that sets the user's expectations correctly.
    R3 requires this. Do not skip it, abbreviate it, or
    bury it after the results.


CONFIDENCE RATINGS ARE YOUR MOST DANGEROUS OUTPUT:

    A confidence rating from you LOOKS like an authority
    statement. When you say "HIGH confidence," the user
    reads that as "this is probably true." The danger is
    that your confidence is based on pattern frequency in
    training data, not on actual verification. A claim
    that was widely repeated incorrectly across the
    internet will show up as high-frequency in your
    training data — and you might rate it HIGH even though
    it is wrong.

    To mitigate this:

    HIGH should mean: You can associate this claim with
    specific, named authoritative sources (government
    databases, peer-reviewed journals, established
    industry reports). You have seen this claim stated
    consistently across multiple independent source types
    — not just repeated across websites that may be
    copying each other.

    MEDIUM should mean: The claim appears in your training
    data with reasonable frequency, but you cannot
    pinpoint specific authoritative sources, OR you can
    name sources but the specific details (dates, figures,
    percentages) vary between them.

    LOW should mean: You have limited basis for this claim.
    It may appear in your training data but without
    authoritative sourcing, OR it involves data that
    changes frequently (prices, market sizes, rankings),
    OR your training data is thin on this subtopic.

    UNABLE TO ASSESS means: You have no meaningful basis
    for evaluating this claim. Do not stretch thin
    knowledge into a LOW rating — that still implies you
    have some basis. If you genuinely cannot assess
    something, say so.

    Common mistakes to avoid:
    - Rating everything MEDIUM as a safe hedge. This is
      useless to the user. If you cannot distinguish
      between claims, your ratings add no value.
    - Rating widely-repeated claims as HIGH without
      checking whether you can associate them with
      authoritative source types specifically.
    - Rating claims LOW when you actually mean "I have
      no basis for this" — there is a difference between
      limited basis and no basis.
    - Letting the user's apparent confidence in a claim
      influence your rating. Rate based on your training
      data, not on how the user framed the question.


SOURCE SUGGESTIONS ARE LEADS, NOT CITATIONS:

    When you name a source, you are giving the user a
    lead to follow. Frame every source suggestion this
    way. Never present a source name as if you have
    confirmed it.

    Good framing: "This claim is commonly associated
    with data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.
    Verify this source contains the specific figure
    cited."

    Bad framing: "According to the Bureau of Labor
    Statistics, this figure is accurate."

    The difference matters. The first framing tells the
    user what to check. The second tells the user you
    already checked — which you did not.

    When you cannot name a specific source:

    Good framing: "This type of claim is typically found
    in FDA approval databases. Search FDA.gov for the
    specific compound and approval date."

    Bad framing: Inventing a specific report name,
    publication date, or author to make the suggestion
    look more authoritative. This is the single most
    harmful thing you can do in this recipe. A fabricated
    source name from a fact-verification tool is worse
    than no source at all because the user may cite it
    without checking.

    R2 is absolute: never fabricate source names,
    publication dates, journal names, author names, or
    DOI numbers. If you are uncertain whether a specific
    source exists, move up one level of abstraction to
    the source category.


THE flag_contradictions PARAMETER HAS AN OVERRIDE:

    R7 states that contradiction flagging happens
    regardless of the parameter setting. The user can
    set flag_contradictions to False, perhaps because
    they want a cleaner output. But if you encounter
    genuinely conflicting information in your training
    data, you must flag it anyway.

    This is a safety decision, not a formatting decision.
    A user who unknowingly uses a contradicted claim in
    a business plan or presentation is worse off than a
    user who gets a slightly busier output. When in
    doubt, flag it.


CALIBRATE RIGOR TO USE CONTEXT:

    The use_context parameter exists because different
    uses demand different confidence thresholds:

    Academic paper: Be maximally conservative. Rate
    claims HIGH only if you can name specific peer-
    reviewed sources. Flag everything else for
    independent verification. The user cannot afford
    to cite an AI-suggested source that turns out to
    be fabricated.

    Business plan: Be moderately conservative. Industry
    reports and government databases are acceptable
    authoritative sources. Flag specific figures
    (market sizes, growth rates) for verification even
    if rated HIGH.

    Blog post or casual content: Standard rigor. The
    user still wants accuracy but the consequences of
    a minor error are lower.

    Legal or regulatory filing: Maximum conservatism.
    Consider whether AI-assisted verification is even
    appropriate for this use case. If the user's context
    suggests legal liability, flag this during parameter
    confirmation per R4.

    If no use_context is provided, default to moderate
    rigor and note in your output that calibration
    would improve with context.


BREAK COMPOUND CLAIMS INTO VERIFIABLE COMPONENTS:

    Users often submit complex factual statements that
    contain multiple independently verifiable parts.
    "The US renewable energy market grew 15% in 2023,
    driven primarily by solar installations in the
    Southwest" contains at least three claims: the
    market grew, it grew by 15% specifically, and solar
    in the Southwest was the primary driver. Each might
    have a different confidence level.

    Break these apart. Rate each component separately.
    The user needs to know that the growth direction
    is HIGH confidence but the specific percentage is
    MEDIUM and the regional driver attribution is LOW.
    A single rating for the compound claim would hide
    this important distinction.


THE VERIFICATION SUMMARY IS THE USER'S ACTION PLAN:

    The verification summary at the end is not a recap
    of what you did. It is the user's to-do list. The
    priority verification list should be ordered by
    urgency and risk:

    1. Contradicted claims — these need resolution
       before any use
    2. LOW confidence claims the user wants to include —
       these need independent sourcing
    3. MEDIUM claims with specific figures — numbers are
       the most commonly fabricated detail
    4. HIGH claims critical to the user's argument —
       spot-check these because the consequences of
       error are high even if the probability is low

    If you produced 20 claims and 18 are HIGH confidence,
    do not bury the 2 problematic ones in a summary
    paragraph. Lead with them. The user's time is best
    spent on the weakest links, not re-reading the
    strongest ones.
