        where a cycle begins or ends, acknowledge that.

        DOCUMENTED — specific event, named entity, published

        ANALYTICAL — pattern identification, cycle framing,
        causal interpretation, trend characterization
        SPECULATIVE — inferences about why something happened,
        connections between events that are interpretive
        Tag each major claim. When in doubt, tag it
        ANALYTICAL rather than DOCUMENTED.

        market share figures, investment amounts, or
        headcounts. If you know a company was founded in

        a year. An approximate date flagged as approximate
        is more valuable than a precise date that may be
        wrong.

        before producing an analysis that looks authoritative
        but is actually thin. Per R2, raise this during
        parameter confirmation.
    
    R7: NEVER impose a cycle framework and then backfill
        evidence to support it. Analyze the events first,

        "comprehensive", analyze ALL four dimensions at
        full depth regardless of priority_dimensions, but

        to the analysis you just produced. Reference actual
        cycles you identified, specific companies you
        mentioned, and dimensions where your confidence was

    # -----------------------------------------------------------
    # I-DONT-KNOW HANDLING
    # -----------------------------------------------------------
    
    This is a hallucination-critical recipe operating at

    KNOWLEDGE GAP PROTOCOL:

    IF your knowledge of different eras within the time

        FLAG which eras are well-documented vs. thin

        MARK claims in thin eras with confidence tags
        DO NOT pad thin eras with generic observations
    
    IF cycle boundaries are genuinely ambiguous:
        PRESENT the boundary you chose AND the alternative
        EXPLAIN what evidence supports each option
        LET the user decide which framing serves their needs
        DO NOT present one framing as the only valid reading
    
    IF quantitative data (market sizes, investment figures,
    adoption rates) is uncertain:
        USE hedging language ("estimated at," "approximately,"

        TAG the claim as ANALYTICAL or SPECULATIVE
        DO NOT present uncertain figures as DOCUMENTED

        Priority Dimensions: {dimensions_display}
    
    SCOPE ASSESSMENT (share with user):
    - Time window adequacy: Does {start_date} to {end_date}

    # -----------------------------------------------------------
    # INNOVATION PATTERN FRAMEWORK

    {end_date}.
    
    DIMENSION 1: INNOVATION CYCLES
    
        Analyze the rhythm of innovation in {industry}:
        
        1.1 MAJOR BREAKTHROUGHS

            - What was the breakthrough?

# ===========================================================
# RCP-011 TLDR RECAP
# Historical Innovation Tracker
# ===========================================================
# For: Website users (human-readable summary)
# Recipe: RCP-000-000-011-HISTORICAL-INNOVATION-TRACKER
# Version: 2.00b
# ===========================================================


WHAT THIS RECIPE DOES:

    It maps how an industry changed over time through
    innovation and disruption. You pick an industry and
    a time window, and the AI produces a structured
    analysis across four dimensions: how breakthroughs
    happened and spread (Innovation Cycles), how the
    competitive landscape shifted (Market Structure), what
    outside forces shaped the industry (External Influences),
    and where money and talent flowed (Resource Allocation).
    The result is a pattern-level view of how an industry
    actually evolved — not just what happened, but the
    rhythm and logic behind the changes.


WHY IT EXISTS:

    Most industry research tells you what happened. This
    recipe tells you how it happened — the patterns,
    the cycles, the recurring dynamics. When you are
    planning an innovation strategy, timing a market
    entry, or trying to understand where an industry is
    headed based on where it has been, you need more than
    a timeline. You need someone to identify the deeper
    patterns: how long do cycles last in this industry,
    what triggers them, what kills incumbents, what
    resources flow in before a breakthrough versus after.
    That is what this recipe does.


WHAT YOU GIVE IT:

    - An industry or sector to analyze
    - A time window (start and end dates — typically
      10 to 30 years to capture multiple cycles)
    - Analysis depth: "focused" (only your priority
      dimensions at full depth) or "comprehensive"
      (all four dimensions at full depth)
    - Optional: which dimensions to prioritize
      (Innovation Cycles, Market Structure, External
      Influences, Resource Allocation)


WHAT YOU GET BACK:

    A multi-dimensional analysis organized by dimension:

    1. Innovation Cycles — major breakthroughs, when
       they happened, who drove them, how fast they
       spread, what resisted them, and how long each
       cycle lasted. Each cycle boundary comes with an
       explanation of why it is placed there.

    2. Market Structure — how the competitive landscape
       changed, which companies rose and fell, when
       consolidation or fragmentation happened, and
       what drove the shifts.

    3. External Influences — economic conditions,
       regulatory changes, and cultural factors that
       shaped the industry during key periods.

    4. Resource Allocation — where venture capital,
       corporate investment, R&D spending, and talent
       flowed over time.

    Then a synthesis section that pulls patterns across
    all four dimensions: what characterized successful
    innovators, what timing factors mattered, what
    failure patterns recurred.

    Every major claim is tagged as Documented (verifiable
    fact), Analytical (pattern interpretation), or
    Speculative (inference), so you always know what is
    history and what is the AI's read on the history.


WHAT MAKES THIS RECIPE DIFFERENT:

    - It checks its own scope before starting. The AI
      tells you if your time window is too short to
      capture meaningful cycles, or if it does not know
      the industry well enough to produce a strong
      analysis.

    - It justifies every cycle boundary. Instead of
      presenting "Cycle 1: 1995-2003" as fact, it
      explains what event or shift marks the start and
      end, and flags if industry analysts would disagree.

    - It does not force neat patterns onto messy history.
      If an industry evolved in overlapping waves rather
      than clean cycles, the AI says so instead of
      inventing artificial boundaries.

    - It has two depth modes. Focused mode gives you
      deep analysis on just the dimensions you care
      about. Comprehensive mode covers everything.

    - It separates facts from interpretation at every
      step, so you know which parts to cite and which
      parts to verify.


BEST USED FOR:

    - Innovation strategy (what patterns in this industry
      should inform my approach?)
    - Market entry timing (where are we in the current
      cycle, based on historical patterns?)
    - Investment planning (what resource flows preceded
      past breakthroughs in this industry?)
    - Competitive positioning (what killed incumbents
      in past cycles, and am I vulnerable to the same?)
    - Product roadmapping (how fast did adoption happen
      in past cycles, and what drove it?)


TIPS FOR BEST RESULTS:

    - Give it enough time to work with. A 5-year window
      might only capture one partial cycle. 15 to 25
      years is the sweet spot for most industries.

    - Be specific about the industry. "Technology" is
      too broad. "Enterprise SaaS" or "consumer mobile
      payments" gives the AI a focused target.

    - Start with comprehensive mode if you are exploring.
      Use focused mode once you know which dimensions
      matter most for your decision.

    - Use the follow-up prompts. The first pass gives
      you the map. Follow-ups let you zoom in on the
      cycles, companies, or dimensions that matter most
      to your specific situation.

    - Pay attention to the confidence tags. The parts
      tagged Analytical and Speculative are where the
      AI is interpreting rather than reporting. Those
      are the parts worth questioning and cross-checking.

AI EXECUTION GUIDANCE:

    You are executing the most complex recipe in the
    Historical Context series. It combines the hallucination
    risks of historical analysis (RCP-009, RCP-010) with
    an additional layer of interpretive framing — you are
    not just reporting what happened, you are identifying
    patterns in what happened. This means every failure
    mode from simpler historical recipes applies here, plus
    a new one: imposing false structure on genuinely messy
    history. Your primary enemies on this recipe are
    fabricated specifics, false precision on cycle
    boundaries, asymmetric era depth disguised as even
    coverage, and pattern imposition masquerading as
    pattern discovery.


CYCLE BOUNDARIES ARE YOUR HIGHEST-RISK OUTPUT:

    When the recipe asks you to identify "distinct
    innovation cycles" with "start and end dates," you
    are being asked to do something fundamentally
    interpretive. There is no objective fact about when
    "Cycle 2 of the software industry" began. Different
    analysts, historians, and industry participants would
    draw the lines differently.

    Your job is not to pretend these boundaries are
    objective. Your job is to propose boundaries, justify
    them, and acknowledge alternatives. Specifically:

    For every cycle you identify, answer these questions
    in the output:
    - What specific event or measurable shift marks the
      start of this cycle?
    - What specific event or measurable shift marks the
      end?
    - Is this a widely recognized periodization, or your
      own analytical framing?
    - Would a reasonable industry analyst draw the line
      in a different place? If so, where and why?

    If the history genuinely does not resolve into clean
    cycles — if innovations overlapped, if transitions
    were gradual rather than punctuated, if the industry
    evolved continuously rather than in phases — then say
    that. R7 exists specifically for this case. An honest
    "this industry shows continuous evolution with
    acceleration points rather than distinct cycles" is a
    better analysis than four fabricated cycles with neat
    date ranges.

    Common mistakes to avoid:
    - Picking round-number years (2000, 2005, 2010) as
      cycle boundaries because they look clean
    - Using product launch dates as cycle boundaries
      when the actual market shift happened years later
    - Creating cycles of suspiciously equal length
    - Forcing exactly 3 or 4 cycles because the template
      structure seems to expect it
    - Presenting your cycle framework as the standard
      industry view when it is your own construction


THE THREE-TIER CONFIDENCE SYSTEM IS NOT DECORATIVE:

    This recipe uses DOCUMENTED, ANALYTICAL, and
    SPECULATIVE rather than the simpler VERIFIED, LIKELY,
    UNCERTAIN system from RCP-010. The reason is that
    innovation analysis involves a higher proportion of
    interpretive claims.

    DOCUMENTED means you are stating a verifiable fact:
    a company was founded, a product launched, a
    regulation passed, a merger closed. These are events
    with dates and named entities that someone could
    confirm with a primary source.

    ANALYTICAL means you are identifying a pattern,
    characterizing a period, proposing a causal
    relationship, or grouping events into a framework.
    "The industry consolidated between 2005 and 2010"
    is ANALYTICAL — it is your characterization of
    multiple documented events, not a single verifiable
    fact. Most of your cycle analysis, pattern synthesis,
    and structural observations will be ANALYTICAL.

    SPECULATIVE means you are making an inference that
    goes beyond the evidence. "Company X likely failed
    because they underestimated market readiness" is
    SPECULATIVE — you are proposing a causal explanation
    that cannot be directly verified from the facts alone.

    Apply these per-claim, not per-section. A single
    paragraph about a company's trajectory might contain
    all three tiers: "Company X was founded in 1998
    [DOCUMENTED], grew rapidly during the first wave of
    enterprise adoption [ANALYTICAL], likely benefiting
    from its early partnership with Y [SPECULATIVE]."

    The user needs this granularity to know which parts
    of your analysis they can cite in a strategy document
    versus which parts require their own validation.


THE analysis_depth PARAMETER IS NOW FUNCTIONAL:

    In the original recipe, "focused" and "comprehensive"
    produced identical output. Now they branch:

    FOCUSED MODE: Analyze only the dimensions listed in
    priority_dimensions at full depth. For the remaining
    dimensions, provide a 2-3 sentence acknowledgment
    that the dimension exists and is relevant, but
    explain it falls outside the focused scope. This is
    not an excuse to skip dimensions — it is a deliberate
    scope choice by the user. Respect it.

    COMPREHENSIVE MODE: Analyze all four dimensions at
    full depth. The priority_dimensions still matter —
    give them additional attention, richer examples, and
    more detailed pattern analysis than the non-priority
    dimensions. But all four dimensions get substantive
    coverage.

    During parameter confirmation (R2), preview what the
    user will get: "In focused mode, I will fully analyze
    Innovation Cycles and Market Structure. External
    Influences and Resource Allocation will receive brief
    summaries only. Would you prefer comprehensive mode
    to cover all four?"


PATTERN SYNTHESIS IS WHERE YOU ADD REAL VALUE:

    The dimensional analyses (Sections 1-4) are research.
    The Pattern Synthesis is where you do actual strategic
    thinking. This section should contain insights that
    only emerge from looking across all the dimensions
    together:

    - Did resource allocation shifts PRECEDE or FOLLOW
      innovation breakthroughs? (leading vs. lagging
      indicator)
    - Did regulatory changes ENABLE or RESPOND TO market
      structure shifts?
    - Were successful innovators concentrated in specific
      market structures (fragmented vs. consolidated)?
    - Did external economic conditions correlate with
      cycle transitions?

    If your Pattern Synthesis could have been written
    without the dimensional analyses, it is restating
    rather than synthesizing. The synthesis should
    surprise the user with connections they would not
    have seen from reading any single dimension alone.


FAILURE PATTERNS DESERVE EQUAL RIGOR:

    Innovation history has severe survivorship bias.
    Your training data contains far more about companies
    that succeeded than companies that failed. This means
    your success analysis will naturally be richer and
    more specific than your failure analysis.

    Do not compensate by fabricating detailed failure
    narratives. Instead:

    - Name specific companies that failed, with whatever
      detail you actually have
    - Flag when your failure data is thinner than your
      success data
    - Explain the likely cause of the gap (survivorship
      bias, limited documentation of failures, smaller
      companies leaving less public record)
    - Provide what you can and mark its confidence level

    "Company X attempted to enter this market in
    approximately 2003 but exited within two years
    [DOCUMENTED]. Likely contributing factors include
    poor timing relative to regulatory changes
    [SPECULATIVE]" is honest and useful. "Multiple
    companies failed due to strategic missteps in the
    mid-2000s" is useless padding.


ERA-DEPTH ASYMMETRY IS DIFFERENT FROM CONTEXT ASYMMETRY:

    RCP-010 deals with asymmetry between two geographic
    or industry contexts. This recipe deals with asymmetry
    across time within a single industry. Your training
    data almost certainly has more detail about recent
    decades than older ones.

    If the user asks for 1980 to 2020, your analysis of
    the 2010s will likely be significantly richer than
    your analysis of the 1980s. Flag this. The user needs
    to know that the apparent acceleration of innovation
    in later decades might partly reflect your data
    density rather than actual industry dynamics.

    Do not compensate by:
    - Padding the 1980s with generic observations
    - Reducing the 2010s to match the 1980s depth
    - Pretending your coverage is even when it is not

    Instead, be transparent: "My analysis of the
    1980-1995 period draws on fewer documented sources
    than the 2000-2020 period. The apparent increase in
    innovation frequency in later decades may partly
    reflect this data asymmetry rather than a genuine
    acceleration."


VALIDATION SUMMARY IS YOUR FINAL SELF-CHECK:

    The VALIDATION SUMMARY is not a formality. Use it
    to genuinely audit your own output:

    - Count your cycle boundaries. Did you justify every
      one? If you wrote "Cycle 2: 2003-2008" somewhere
      without explaining what happened in 2003 and 2008
      to mark those boundaries, fix it before finalizing.
    - Count your confidence tags. If 90% of your claims
      are tagged DOCUMENTED, you are probably under-
      tagging your interpretive content. Pattern analysis
      should produce a healthy proportion of ANALYTICAL
      claims.
    - Check your depth compliance. If the user asked for
      focused mode and you wrote 500 words on a non-
      priority dimension, you overscoped.
    - List your knowledge gaps honestly. This is your
      last chance to flag thin areas before the user
      treats your analysis as comprehensive.
