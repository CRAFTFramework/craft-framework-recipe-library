"""
TLDR RECAP: Discussion Synthesis & Strategic Alignment

WHAT IT DOES:
Converts stakeholder simulation findings into an actionable
engagement strategy. Takes the Discussion Transcript from
RCP-082 and produces a 13-section Strategic Alignment Report
that maps alignment, identifies blocking issues, recommends
engagement sequence, and generates testable action steps.

HOW IT WORKS:
You provide the Discussion Transcript from your stakeholder
simulation (from RCP-082). The AI systematically analyzes
where stakeholders aligned and where they diverged, identifies
blocking issues and negotiation opportunities, maps alliances
and opposition patterns, develops a recommended engagement
sequence with specific rationale, and creates stakeholder-
specific communication strategies. Everything is grounded
in simulation evidence and framed as hypotheses to test.

WHAT TO EXPECT:
This is NOT a prediction or guarantee of engagement outcomes.
The report gives you a map based on simulation findings and
a starting plan for real conversations. It explicitly
acknowledges what the simulation could NOT capture (personal
relationships, political dynamics, informal influence
networks, recent events). Your first real conversation will
teach you more than the entire simulation.

BEST RESULTS WHEN YOU:
- Provide a detailed Discussion Transcript from RCP-082
- Bring real-world constraints (organizational politics,
  budget cycles, relationship dynamics)
- Use the engagement sequence and action steps as testable
  hypotheses, not certainties
- Adjust strategy after each real stakeholder conversation
- Review surprise findings for mental model updates

WORKFLOW POSITION:
Recipe 4 of 4 in the Stakeholder Analysis Workflow:
  RCP-080 (Stakeholder Map) →
  DR (Deep Research) →
  RCP-081 (Persona File) →
  RCP-082 (Discussion Simulation) →
  RCP-083 (This Recipe - Strategic Alignment Report)

TIME: 20-40 minutes for full report synthesis
DIFFICULTY: Intermediate — analyze simulation findings and
  convert to actionable strategy
"""

# ITEM 2: EXTENDED AI GUIDANCE
# ===========================================================

"""
EXTENDED AI GUIDANCE: Discussion Synthesis & Strategic Alignment

PURPOSE OF THIS GUIDANCE:
This section provides supplementary direction for AI
platforms executing RCP-083. It covers synthesis methodology,
report quality standards, and the critical simulation-to-
reality transition.


CORE PHILOSOPHY:

RCP-083 is the BRIDGE between simulation and reality. It
converts structured thought experiments into actionable
engagement plans. Every recommendation is a hypothesis —
the user walks out with testable assumptions, not false
certainty.

Think of the AI as a post-game analyst who reviews the
footage and prepares the next game plan, knowing the
actual game will be different.


SYNTHESIS METHODOLOGY:

EVIDENCE HIERARCHY:
    The report should prioritize evidence by strength:
    1. Positions stated multiple times across rounds
       (consistent, likely durable in reality)
    2. Positions stated once with supporting reasoning
       (credible but less tested)
    3. Positions inferred from reactions or silence
       (weakest — flag as inference)

    When a persona shifted position during the discussion,
    note BOTH the original and final positions. The shift
    itself is data.

BLOCKING ISSUE IDENTIFICATION:
    A true blocking issue requires three elements:
    1. A stakeholder with sufficient influence to halt progress
    2. An unresolved objection to a core element of the proposal
    3. No resolution path demonstrated in the simulation

    If ANY of these is missing, it's a concern, not a block.
    Overstating concerns as blocking issues creates false
    urgency. Understating true blocks creates blind spots.

ENGAGEMENT SEQUENCE LOGIC:
    The sequence is the report's highest-value output.
    Common sequencing strategies:

    Convert-blockers-first: Engage potential blockers early,
    convert them to supporters or co-designers before they
    can organize opposition.

    Build-coalition-first: Engage natural supporters to
    build momentum, then approach skeptics from a position
    of strength.

    Address-dependencies: Some stakeholders need information
    from others. Engage information providers first.

    The right strategy depends on the specific dynamics.
    State which strategy you're using and why.


REPORT SECTION GUIDANCE:

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:
    2-3 sentences maximum. "We simulated [topic] with
    [N] stakeholders over [N] rounds. Key finding:
    [one-sentence takeaway]. Primary path forward:
    [one-sentence recommendation]."

STAKEHOLDER LANDSCAPE:
    For each stakeholder, capture: where they ended
    (not just where they started), what moved them
    (or didn't), and what remains unresolved. This
    section answers "where do we stand with each person?"

ALIGNMENT MAP:
    Visual-friendly format. Group stakeholders by position:
    supportive / conditional / opposed / undetermined.
    Note cross-group connections. This should be scannable.

BLOCKING ISSUES:
    Prioritize ruthlessly. Most simulations reveal 1-3
    true blocking issues. If you have more than 5, you're
    either overstating concerns or the proposal needs
    fundamental rethinking (which is also a valid finding).

COMMUNICATION STRATEGY:
    Be specific. "Lead with data" is too vague. "Open with
    the SHRM retention study (cited in simulation Round 2),
    frame as investment with defined payback period, avoid
    'everyone is doing it' framing (triggered skepticism
    in Round 3)" is actionable.

REAL-WORLD ACTION STEPS:
    This is the most important section. Each action should
    be a specific, testable hypothesis:

    Good: "Ask CFO: 'If we co-designed the evaluation
    metrics, would you support a 6-month pilot?' Listen
    for whether they engage with the specifics or deflect
    to broader concerns."

    Bad: "Discuss pilot metrics with finance leadership."

    The distinction is specificity. The user should know
    exactly what to say, what to ask, and what to listen for.


SIMULATION-TO-REALITY TRANSITION:

This is the hardest and most important part of the recipe.
The user has spent time in a structured simulation and may
have developed confidence in the outcomes. The report must
CALIBRATE that confidence.

WHAT SIMULATION CAPTURES WELL:
    - Logical structure of stakeholder arguments
    - Which concerns are predictable given role and evidence
    - Where flexibility zones MIGHT exist
    - Which proposals address multiple concerns simultaneously

WHAT SIMULATION MISSES:
    - Personal relationships and trust levels
    - Informal influence networks
    - Recent events that changed dynamics
    - Emotional responses to specific framings
    - Political calculations beyond the immediate decision
    - Information stakeholders have that wasn't in DR

The closing section should be clear: "Your first real
conversation will teach you more than the entire simulation.
Be prepared to update your strategy after every interaction."


EDGE CASES:

SIMULATION ENDED WITHOUT RESOLUTION:
    This is a VALID finding. The report should say: "The
    simulation did not produce consensus, which suggests
    this decision may require phased or conditional
    approaches rather than full-group agreement."

ALL STAKEHOLDERS AGREED:
    Be suspicious. Check whether artificial consensus
    crept in during simulation. If genuine agreement
    existed, the report should note: "Broad alignment
    suggests the proposal may succeed, but TEST this
    assumption — simulation tends to underrepresent
    hidden objections."

TRANSCRIPT IS THIN (few rounds):
    Work with what you have. Note in limitations that
    the simulation was brief and deeper exploration
    might reveal additional dynamics. Recommend specific
    areas where additional simulation rounds would help.

USER PROVIDES EXTENSIVE REAL-WORLD CONSTRAINTS:
    Incorporate them into the engagement sequence and
    communication strategy. Real-world constraints often
    change the optimal engagement order.


QUALITY STANDARDS:

REPORT QUALITY CHECKLIST:
    [ ] Every recommendation cites simulation evidence
    [ ] No fabricated precision (percentages, probabilities)
    [ ] Hypothesis framing throughout
    [ ] Blocking issues limited to genuine blocks
    [ ] Engagement sequence has rationale for each position
    [ ] Communication strategies are specific and actionable
    [ ] Action steps include what to say, ask, and listen for
    [ ] Simulation limitations explicitly acknowledged
    [ ] Surprise findings highlighted
    [ ] Report is self-contained (readable independently)
    [ ] Closing transitions from simulation to real world
    [ ] No new stakeholder intelligence introduced
"""

# BEHAVIORAL RULES
# ===========================================================

"""
BEHAVIORAL RULES for RCP-083 execution (from WPRM):

RULE 1 — SIMULATION-GROUNDED ONLY:
  Every recommendation must be traceable to something
  that happened in the simulation. Do not introduce new
  stakeholder intelligence, industry analysis, or
  strategic frameworks beyond what the discussion
  produced. If a recommendation isn't supported by
  simulation evidence, don't make it. Cite specific
  rounds, statements, or dynamics as evidence.

RULE 2 — HYPOTHESIS FRAMING:
  All strategic recommendations are HYPOTHESES to test
  with real stakeholders, not conclusions. Frame every
  recommendation as: "Based on the simulation, [finding].
  Test this by [action]." The user should leave this
  recipe with testable assumptions, not false certainty.

RULE 3 — ENGAGEMENT SEQUENCE RATIONALE:
  The recommended engagement sequence must explain WHY
  each stakeholder should be engaged in that order.
  "Start with Finance" needs a reason: "because
  converting them from blocker to co-architect provides
  the metrics framework others will need." Order matters
  and the rationale should reference simulation dynamics.

RULE 4 — HONEST ABOUT SIMULATION LIMITS:
  The report must explicitly acknowledge what the
  simulation COULD NOT capture: personal relationships,
  political dynamics not reflected in personas, recent
  events, emotional context, informal influence networks.
  Real stakeholder engagement will always reveal factors
  the simulation missed.

RULE 5 — STAKEHOLDER-SPECIFIC COMMUNICATION:
  Communication strategy recommendations should reflect
  each stakeholder's communication style from the persona
  file AND their behavior during the simulation. If
  Finance responded well to data in the simulation,
  recommend a data-led approach. If Client Success
  needed concrete proposals, recommend leading with
  specifics.

RULE 6 — BLOCKING ISSUE PRIORITIZATION:
  Not all disagreements are blocking issues. A blocking
  issue is one where a stakeholder with sufficient
  influence to halt progress holds an unresolved
  objection. Distinguish between: blocking issues
  (must resolve), concerns (should address), and
  preferences (nice to accommodate). Prioritize
  blocking issues in the action plan.

RULE 7 — ALLIANCE MAPPING:
  Identify which stakeholders aligned during the
  simulation and on what basis. Natural alliances
  (shared interests) are stronger than tactical
  alliances (temporary alignment). Map both. Note
  which alliances could be leveraged in real-world
  engagement and which might be fragile.

RULE 8 — RISK MAPPING:
  For each blocking issue, identify: who could block,
  what triggers the block, what mitigates it, and what
  happens if it's not resolved. Risk levels should be
  grounded in simulation evidence, not generic risk
  frameworks. "Finance will block if no metrics
  framework is presented" is simulation-grounded.
  "There is a 60% risk of organizational resistance"
  is fabricated precision.

RULE 9 — ACTIONABLE OUTPUT:
  The final section must convert ALL findings into
  concrete, testable actions. Each action should be
  specific enough that the user could act on it
  tomorrow: who to talk to, what to propose, what
  question to ask, what to listen for. Abstract
  recommendations ("build coalition support") are
  insufficient without specifics.

RULE 10 — REAL-WORLD TRANSITION:
  The closing section must explicitly acknowledge the
  transition from simulation to reality: "This report
  gives you a map and a starting plan. The first real
  conversation will update that map. Be prepared to
  adjust." Frame the report as a living starting point,
  not a finished strategy.

RULE 11 — NO FALSE PRECISION:
  Do not assign percentages, probability scores,
  success likelihoods, or numerical confidence levels
  to engagement outcomes. "Finance is likely to support
  a pilot with metrics" is appropriate. "There is a
  75% chance Finance supports the pilot" is not.
  Qualitative assessments only.

RULE 12 — SURPRISE FINDINGS:
  Highlight any simulation outcomes that were unexpected
  based on initial persona definitions. If a stakeholder
  showed flexibility where their persona predicted
  rigidity, or vice versa, call it out. Surprises from
  simulation are the most valuable findings because they
  suggest the user's mental model may need updating.
"""
