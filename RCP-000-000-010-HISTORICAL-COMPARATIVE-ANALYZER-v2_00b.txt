WHAT THIS RECIPE DOES:

    It compares how the same topic played out in two
    different places, industries, or time periods. You
    pick a subject — like "e-commerce" or "renewable
    energy regulation" — choose two contexts to compare,
    set a time window, and the AI produces a structured
    side-by-side historical analysis covering key events,
    market dynamics, regulations, successes, and failures
    in both contexts.


WHY IT EXISTS:

    When you are expanding into a new market, evaluating
    a cross-border strategy, or just trying to understand
    why the same trend went differently in two places,
    you need more than a summary of each place separately.
    You need someone to actually compare them — find the
    patterns, explain the divergences, and tell you what
    lessons transfer and what does not. That is what this
    recipe does.


WHAT YOU GIVE IT:

    - A topic (what you want to compare)
    - A primary context (your home market, industry, or era)
    - A secondary context (the comparison target)
    - A time window (start and end dates)
    - Optional: which dimensions to focus on (market,
      regulatory, technology, or others)


WHAT YOU GET BACK:

    A four-section analysis:

    1. How the topic developed in your primary context —
       timeline, market dynamics, regulations, technology.

    2. How the same topic developed in the secondary
       context during the same period.

    3. A direct comparison — what was similar, what
       diverged, why, and detailed success and failure
       cases from each side.

    4. Cross-context insights — which success factors
       transfer, which pitfalls are universal, and what
       is unique to each context.

    Every major claim is tagged with a confidence level
    (Verified, Likely, or Uncertain) so you know which
    parts you can cite directly and which ones you should
    double-check.


WHAT MAKES THIS RECIPE DIFFERENT:

    - It confirms your scope before diving in, so you
      do not waste time on a misaligned analysis.

    - It tells you when it knows more about one context
      than the other, instead of quietly producing a
      lopsided comparison.

    - It separates documented facts from analytical
      interpretation, so you always know what is history
      and what is the AI's read on the history.

    - It enforces a hard time boundary — no events
      outside your specified window sneak in.

    - The follow-up suggestions at the end are specific
      to your actual results, not generic prompts.


BEST USED FOR:

    - Market expansion research (how did X play out
      in the country I am entering?)
    - Cross-border strategy (what regulatory differences
      should I plan for?)
    - Investment due diligence (how did similar markets
      develop at different stages?)
    - Product adaptation (what localization lessons can
      I learn from history?)
    - Risk assessment (what failed elsewhere that I
      should watch for here?)


TIPS FOR BEST RESULTS:

    - Pick contexts that are genuinely comparable. Comparing
      US fintech to EU fintech is strong. Comparing US
      fintech to 18th-century maritime trade is not.

    - Keep the time window focused. 10 to 20 years works
      well. 50 years is usually too broad for useful
      comparison.

    - Be specific about your topic. "Technology adoption"
      is vague. "Mobile payment adoption in retail" gives
      the AI something concrete to work with.

    - Use the follow-up prompts. The first pass gives you
      breadth. Follow-ups give you depth on the parts
      that matter most to your decision.

    comparison frameworks that produce low-quality output.
    During confirmation, actively evaluate whether the
    comparison is viable. If the user asks to compare
    "technology in Asia vs Europe from 1900 to 2020," that
    is too broad on every axis — region, topic, and time.
    Say so. Suggest a refinement. The ten seconds spent
    narrowing scope saves the user from a shallow 2,000-word
    analysis they cannot use.


THE CONFIDENCE TAGGING SYSTEM IS STRUCTURAL:

    The three tiers (VERIFIED, LIKELY, UNCERTAIN) are not
    decorative. Apply them per-claim, not per-section.
    Within a single timeline, some events will be VERIFIED
    (Amazon launched in 1995) and others UNCERTAIN (the
    exact market share shift in a specific quarter). Do not
    batch-tag an entire section as one confidence level.

    Common mistakes to avoid:
    - Tagging everything VERIFIED because it "sounds right"
    - Tagging everything LIKELY as a hedge to avoid commitment
    - Forgetting to tag quantitative claims (market sizes,
      growth rates, adoption percentages are the claims
      most likely to be fabricated and most in need of
      confidence tagging)


ASYMMETRIC DEPTH IS YOUR BIGGEST CREDIBILITY RISK:

    Your training data is not uniformly distributed across
    geographies, languages, industries, and time periods.
    You almost certainly know more about US markets than
    Peruvian markets, more about English-language regulatory
    frameworks than Mandarin-language ones, more about the
    2010s than the 1970s. This is not a flaw to hide. It
    is a fact to disclose.

    When you notice the asymmetry (and you will — one
    section will flow easily while the other requires more
    hedging), trigger R4 immediately. The user would rather
    know "I have 12 well-documented milestones for the US
    context but only 4 for the Brazilian context on this
    topic" than receive a comparison where one side is
    rich with specifics and the other is padded with
    generic observations dressed up as analysis.

    Do not equalize depth by:
    - Adding vague filler ("the market grew steadily")
    - Repackaging the same point in different words
    - Substituting regional generalizations for specific
      country-level data
    - Inventing plausible-sounding organizations or dates


THE TIME BOUNDARY IS A HARD WALL:

    R1 is absolute. If the user sets an end date of 2015,
    you may not reference events from 2016 even if they
    are directly relevant and would strengthen the analysis.
    This is a deliberate design choice: the user is using
    historical analysis to inform decisions, and mixing in
    events they may already know about from recent memory
    undermines the purpose. If post-boundary events are
    critical, note in your follow-up suggestions that the
    user may want to extend the analysis window, but do
    not preemptively include them.

    Similarly, do not anchor your analysis to your knowledge
    of how things eventually turned out. If analyzing
    e-commerce from 2000 to 2010, do not let your knowledge
    of 2024 outcomes color how you describe 2005 dynamics.
    Analyze the period as it was, not through the lens of
    what came later.


COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS IS NOT TWO SUMMARIES SIDE BY SIDE:

    Sections 1 and 2 are individual context analyses.
    Section 3 is where the actual comparison happens. The
    most common failure here is producing Section 3 as a
    superficial overlay that restates what was already said
    in Sections 1 and 2. Section 3 should contain NEW
    analytical insight that only emerges from the juxtaposition:

    - Timing gaps (Context A adopted 5 years before B — why?)
    - Causal differences (same outcome, different drivers)
    - Structural divergences (Context A was market-led,
      Context B was regulation-led)
    - Transferability judgments (this success factor was
      context-dependent vs. this one is portable)

    If Section 3 could be generated without having written
    Sections 1 and 2, it is not doing its job.


FAILURE CASES REQUIRE THE SAME RIGOR AS SUCCESS CASES:

    There is a tendency to provide detailed, specific
    success cases and then offer vague, generic failure
    cases. Both require named organizations, specific
    actions, identifiable causes, and (where available)
    quantifiable consequences. "Several companies failed
    to adapt" is not a failure case. "Company X launched
    initiative Y in [year], which failed because of Z,
    resulting in [consequence]" is a failure case.

    If you genuinely cannot identify specific failure cases
    for a context, say so and explain why (limited
    documentation, survivorship bias in available sources,
    or gaps in your training data). Do not fabricate
    failure narratives to fill the template.


FOLLOW-UP SUGGESTIONS MUST BE EARNED:

    R8 requires dynamic follow-ups. After completing the
    analysis, review what you actually produced and
    generate follow-up questions that address:

    1. The most interesting divergence — the place where
       the two contexts went most differently and the
       user might want to understand why in more depth.

    2. Your lowest-confidence area — where you had to
       hedge the most, giving the user a chance to ask
       you to dig deeper or flag it for their own research.

    3. A specific case worth expanding — an organization,
       regulation, or event that was mentioned briefly but
       has a richer story behind it.

    4. The asymmetry gap — if one context was thinner,
       suggest the user ask specifically about that context
       to see if targeted prompting surfaces more detail.

    Do not recycle the generic suggestions from the
    original recipe. Every follow-up should reference
    something specific from THIS analysis.


VALIDATION SUMMARY IS A SELF-AUDIT:

    The VALIDATION SUMMARY at the end is not cosmetic.
    Actually count your milestones per context. Actually
    tally your confidence distribution. If the summary
    reveals a 15-to-4 milestone imbalance you did not
    flag earlier, that is a problem — go back and add the
    R4 asymmetry disclosure. The summary exists to catch
    your own oversights before the user has to.
