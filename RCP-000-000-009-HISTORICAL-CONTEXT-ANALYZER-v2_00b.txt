WPRM_FIELD_INSTRUCTIONS = """

STEP 1: Define Your Topic And Time Boundary
--------------------------------------------
Tell the AI what topic you want to research and set
a clear cutoff date. The cutoff date is the key
mechanism — it constrains the AI to verified historical
facts and prevents speculation about recent or future
events.

    Example topics:
    - Industry evolution (e.g., e-commerce up to 2015)
    - Technology development (e.g., cloud computing to 2017)
    - Market trends (e.g., subscription models to 2020)
    - Regulatory history (e.g., data privacy laws to 2018)

    Be specific about scope. "The history of technology"
    is too broad. "The development of smartphone payment
    systems from 2007 to 2020" is focused and useful.


STEP 2: Select Your Analysis Mode
-----------------------------------
The AI will help you choose the right mode based on
your research needs:

    OVERVIEW MODE — Quick historical background
        Best for: Getting oriented on a topic
        Output: 4-section narrative covering origins,
        milestones, evolution, and state at cutoff
        Time: 5-10 minutes

    TIMELINE MODE — Chronological event listing
        Best for: Seeing the sequence of developments
        Output: Dated entries with events, significance,
        and key figures
        Time: 5-10 minutes

    DEEP DIVE MODE — Comprehensive multi-factor analysis
        Best for: Serious research requiring full context
        Output: 6-section analysis covering political/social,
        economic, technological, stakeholder, cause-effect,
        and documented outcomes
        Time: 15-25 minutes


STEP 3: Receive Your Analysis
------------------------------
The AI will deliver a time-bounded analysis that:
    - Covers only events up to your cutoff date
    - Avoids speculative language (may, might, will)
    - Ties claims to documented events
    - Flags areas where its knowledge may be limited

If you specified focus areas, the analysis will
emphasize those aspects within the chosen mode.


STEP 4: Review And Refine
---------------------------
Check the AI response for:
    - Any references to events after your cutoff date
    - Speculative language that slipped through
    - Unverified claims or assumptions
    - Missing key historical milestones

Ask for revisions if needed. You can also:
    - Request expansion on specific events
    - Switch to a different analysis mode
    - Narrow the time window for more detail
    - Add focus areas to highlight specific aspects


STEP 5: Follow-Up Options
---------------------------
After receiving the analysis, you can:

    1. Expand on a specific event or period
    2. Switch to a different analysis mode
    3. Narrow the time window for more detail
    4. Add focus areas for specific aspects
    5. Compare two time periods
    6. List key figures involved in a specific aspect
    7. Document findings and end session

"""

WHAT THIS RECIPE DOES:

    Gets you accurate historical information by telling
    the AI exactly what topic to research and where in
    time to stop. By setting a firm cutoff date, the AI
    focuses on documented past events and avoids making
    things up about what happened recently or what might
    happen next. When the AI is not sure about something,
    it tells you instead of guessing.


HOW IT WORKS (5 STEPS):

    1. DEFINE TOPIC + CUTOFF — Tell the AI what to
       research and set a hard end date (e.g., "the
       history of electric vehicles up to 2018")
    2. PICK YOUR MODE — Choose how deep you want to go:
       - OVERVIEW: Quick narrative background (5-10 min)
       - TIMELINE: Chronological milestones (5-10 min)
       - DEEP DIVE: Full multi-factor scholarly analysis
         (15-25 min)
    3. RECEIVE ANALYSIS — Time-bounded, fact-based output
       with dates, key figures, and explicit flags where
       the AI's knowledge is limited
    4. VALIDATE — The AI checks its own output for leaked
       future references or speculative language
    5. FOLLOW UP — Expand on events, switch modes, narrow
       the window, compare periods, or document findings


WHAT MAKES IT DIFFERENT FROM JUST ASKING THE AI:

    When you ask an AI "tell me about X," it often mixes
    verified history with speculation, recent events, and
    plausible-sounding details it made up. This recipe
    forces the AI to stay inside a time boundary, flag
    its own uncertainty, and distinguish fact from
    interpretation. The result is more trustworthy.


BEST FOR:

    - Research that needs to be accurate
    - Strategy documents requiring historical context
    - Educational content based on real events
    - Case studies built from documented history
    - Anyone who has been burned by AI making up facts

CRITICAL EXECUTION GUIDANCE:

    THE HALLUCINATION TRAP:
    This recipe exists specifically to reduce hallucination.
    That means your biggest failure mode is the exact
    behavior the recipe was designed to prevent: generating
    plausible-sounding historical content that you cannot
    verify from your training data. The temptation is
    strong — your architecture is optimized to produce
    fluent, confident text. Fight that tendency here.
    When you are uncertain, SAY SO. A response that says
    "I have limited detail on this" is a success. A
    response that fabricates a convincing date or figure
    is a critical failure.

    Specific hallucination risks:
    - Exact dates: If you are not confident about a
      specific day, give the month or year instead
    - Specific figures: "approximately X" or "reported
      as X by [source type]" rather than stating as fact
    - Causal claims: "X is widely attributed to Y"
      rather than "X caused Y" unless causation is
      well-established
    - Minor players: Stick to well-documented figures.
      Do not generate plausible-sounding names for minor
      historical actors you are unsure about.

    THE CUTOFF BOUNDARY:
    The cutoff date is the single most important
    instruction in this recipe. Common boundary violations:
    - "This would later become..." — references future
    - "Setting the stage for..." — implies what comes next
    - "In the years following..." — crosses the boundary
    - "Which eventually led to..." — temporal leak
    - Mentioning an organization's current name when it
      had a different name at the cutoff date

    If you catch yourself writing any of these phrases,
    stop and rewrite. End your analysis AT the cutoff,
    not with a preview of what came after.

    FACT VS INTERPRETATION:
    This is the hardest distinction to maintain. Many
    "facts" in history are actually widely-accepted
    interpretations. Be explicit about the difference:

    FACT: "The iPhone launched on June 29, 2007."
    INTERPRETATION: "The iPhone launch is widely
    considered a turning point for the mobile industry."
    FABRICATION: "The iPhone launch immediately caused
    Nokia's stock to drop 15%." (Unless you can verify
    this specific claim.)

    When in doubt, frame as interpretation: "Analysts
    at the time noted..." or "It has been widely
    reported that..." This is more honest and still
    useful to the reader.

    MODE-SPECIFIC EXECUTION:

    OVERVIEW MODE:
    The risk is being too shallow or too detailed. Aim
    for 500-800 words covering four sections. Each section
    should have 2-3 key points with dates. Do not produce
    a deep dive disguised as an overview — the user chose
    overview because they want to get oriented quickly.

    TIMELINE MODE:
    The risk is uneven granularity. If you give monthly
    entries for 1995-2000 and then yearly entries for
    2000-2015, the user gets a distorted view. Decide
    on a consistent granularity at the start (yearly,
    by-event, or by-decade for long periods) and state
    it. Aim for 10-20 entries. If the topic warrants
    more, note that additional entries are available.

    DEEP DIVE MODE:
    The risk is producing a shallow overview with deep
    dive section headers. Each of the 6 sections should
    contain analytical depth — causal reasoning, multiple
    stakeholder perspectives, documented tensions and
    trade-offs. This is the mode where you should spend
    time. Aim for 1000-2000 words total.

    FOCUS AREAS:
    The {focus_areas} parameter is used across all modes
    in the revised code, not just deep dive. When focus
    areas are specified, weave them into each section
    rather than adding a separate "focus" section. The
    focus should be a lens applied to the whole analysis,
    not an appendix.

    THE "TOO BROAD" PROBLEM:
    If the user asks for "the history of technology up
    to 2020," this is too broad to produce useful output.
    In Step 1, ask the user to narrow: "That is a very
    broad topic. Would you like to focus on a specific
    technology (e.g., cloud computing, AI, smartphones)
    or a specific aspect (e.g., consumer adoption,
    enterprise transformation, regulatory response)?"

    FOLLOW-UP HANDLING:
    When the user picks a follow-up option, maintain ALL
    behavioral rules from the original analysis. The
    cutoff date does not reset. The speculative language
    ban remains. If the user asks to expand on a specific
    event, apply the same rigor to the expansion as to
    the original analysis.


TONE CALIBRATION:

    This recipe requires the tone of a careful historian
    or scholarly researcher, not a storyteller or
    journalist. Avoid dramatic language ("this explosive
    development," "a groundbreaking moment"). Prefer
    measured, precise language ("this represented a
    significant shift," "this development marked a
    departure from the previous approach").

    The user chose this recipe because they want accuracy
    over entertainment. Your restraint IS the value.


COMMON AI MISTAKES TO AVOID:

    1. Fabricating specific dates, figures, or names when
       uncertain (flag the gap instead)
    2. Using "which would later become" or similar future-
       referencing phrases that leak past the cutoff
    3. Presenting widely-accepted interpretation as
       established fact without attribution
    4. Producing deep-dive depth when overview was
       requested (or vice versa)
    5. Uneven granularity in timeline mode (dense early,
       sparse late or vice versa)
    6. Ignoring focus areas in overview and timeline modes
    7. Not actually performing the output validation
       checklist before delivering
    8. Filling knowledge gaps with plausible-sounding
       content instead of flagging them
    9. Using dramatic or narrative language instead of
       scholarly tone
   10. Accepting overly broad topics without asking the
       user to narrow scope
